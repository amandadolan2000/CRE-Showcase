The AAA–Class A–Class B/C Bifurcation

Toronto's downtown office market is operating as two distinct markets in parallel. Trophy and AAA product — PATH-connected, post-2015 vintage, floorplates above 20,000 SF — is experiencing near-full occupancy at 7.2% vacancy. Demand is driven by financial services, legal, and public sector tenants making long-term commitments to top-quality, amenitized space. Rents at this tier have held firm and in some cases grown, with CIBC Square I (81 Bay) and TD Centre continuing to transact in the $42–$55/SF range.

Class A product from the 1980s–2000s vintage — well-located but less amenitized, smaller floorplates, higher operating costs — faces structural headwinds. Vacancy at this tier sits at 15.6%, compressed by sublease activity and tenants downsizing on renewal. The flight-to-quality trend accelerated post-pandemic and is now deeply embedded in occupier strategy, particularly among law firms and financial services firms right-sizing portfolios.

Class B/C vacancy has widened to 24–28%, with conversion to residential a growing theme for obsolete product. This tier has limited investment market relevance for institutional buyers.

70 York Street — Market Positioning

70 York occupies a compelling middle position in this bifurcated market. Comprehensively repositioned over 2022–2024, it presents with AAA-adjacent physical quality in a PATH-connected Financial District location, while trading at a cap rate consistent with Class A rather than trophy pricing (6.50% vs. a theoretical AAA range of 5.00–5.50%). This basis reflects three factors: residual vintage discount (original 1970s construction), the remaining 7.5% vacancy, and the relative illiquidity of sub-$200M office transactions in the current rate environment.

The CMHC anchor (22% of RBA, Dec 2033 expiry) is the single most important income quality feature of the asset. Federal government covenant eliminates credit risk on the largest tenancy for the majority of the hold period. The Desjardins co-purchaser/tenant relationship adds an additional layer of income certainty, albeit with the governance considerations noted in the lease abstraction.

The positive net absorption of +2.65M SF in 2025 — the first consecutive year of positive absorption in the downtown core since 2019 — represents a genuine inflection point. The sublease overhang has been meaningfully reduced (from 44% to 20.7% of vacancy), which is a leading indicator of rental rate recovery at the Class A tier. For a well-located, repositioned asset like 70 York, this absorption trend supports the underwriting assumption of lease-up of the vacant suite within six months at or near $30/SF.

Investment Market Context — Toronto Office Capital Markets

Transaction velocity in Toronto office declined materially in 2023–2024 as buyers and sellers struggled to agree on pricing amid rising rates. The 70 York trade represents one of the more significant Financial District office transactions in 2025, and serves as a market-setting data point for stabilised, repositioned product in the $100–$200M range.

At a 6.50% going-in cap rate and $639/SF, the pricing reflects a meaningful discount to replacement cost (estimated $900–$1,100/SF for new Class A construction in the core) while offering institutional-quality income from day one. The buyer profile — Desjardins DGAM (Quebec pension capital) and an undisclosed pension co-investor — is consistent with the return of patient, long-duration institutional capital to Toronto office, particularly at basis levels offering downside protection.

CBRE's Canadian Cap Rate Survey (H2 2025) places stabilised Class A Financial District office in the 5.75–6.75% range, with core trophy assets transacting tighter. At 6.50%, 70 York trades at the midpoint of this range — reflective of its repositioned quality offset by residual vintage and vacancy risk.

Analyst Commentary — Positioning vs. Comp Set

Pricing at $639/SF: The subject trades materially above the Class A comparable average of ~$490/SF, reflecting the comprehensive repositioning program, PATH connectivity, government-anchored tenancy, and strong WALE of 6.0 years. The premium to 55 University Avenue ($522/SF, 6.75% cap) and 1 Queen Street East ($554/SF, 6.25% cap) is attributable to the higher occupancy (92.5% vs. 85–88%), superior tenant covenant mix, and more recent capital program. On a per-SF basis, the 70 York pricing is more directly comparable to 25 York Street ($661/SF, Class A+ South Core), though that transaction occurred in a materially lower rate environment (Q4 2022).

Cap Rate at 6.50%: The 6.50% going-in cap rate is broadly consistent with the current market for stabilised, repositioned Class A product with institutional-quality tenancy. It represents a 100–150 bps spread to the AAA tier (Scotia Plaza / 25 York at 5.00–5.50%) and a 25–50 bps discount to lower-occupancy or non-renovated comparable assets (20 Queen at ~8.50%, 200 King at ~7.25%). This positioning is rational — the asset is meaningfully better than the distressed comp set but is not yet commanding trophy pricing, which requires a longer track record of stabilised performance and a fully leased building.

Below-Replacement-Cost Basis: At $639/SF, Desjardins and its pension co-investor acquired at an estimated 40–45% discount to replacement cost for new Class A construction in the core (~$900–$1,100/SF all-in). This embedded margin of safety is a critical downside protection for an office investment in the current cycle — even in a scenario where cap rates widen 50 bps on exit, the basis provides meaningful buffer.

Takeaway: The 70 York transaction is consistent with a reopening of the Toronto office investment market for well-located, repositioned product with strong institutional tenancy. It confirms that patient, long-duration capital will deploy at pricing that offers a genuine discount to replacement cost and a spread to government bonds, while avoiding the distressed end of the market. This transaction will likely serve as a key benchmark comp for Financial District office trades in 2026.
