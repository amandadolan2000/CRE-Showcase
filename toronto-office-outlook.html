01 — The Central Thesis
Our Call
A Tale of Two Markets
Toronto's downtown office market enters 2026 with genuine momentum — but that momentum is not evenly distributed. AAA and well-positioned Class A product is in a supply-constrained upcycle. Class B/C is experiencing structural obsolescence that no amount of rent concession will fully reverse. Three forces are converging to make 2026 the year this bifurcation becomes permanent: the disappearance of new supply, accelerating RTO mandates pulling tenants toward quality, and large-block scarcity forcing major occupiers to make decisions now or lose access to premium space entirely. For investors, the window to acquire stabilised downtown Toronto office at historically wide cap rates is closing. For Class B/C landlords, the path forward runs through repositioning or conversion — not leasing recovery.

Why This Matters The headline vacancy number of 15.0% obscures the reality of what is actually two distinct markets operating under the same label. A recruiter, investor, or occupier looking at Toronto CBD office through an aggregate lens will systematically misread the opportunity. The insight that matters in 2026 is not where the average is — it's understanding the gap between AAA at 3.0% and Class B/C at 25.4%, and what structural forces will drive that gap wider over the next 12–24 months.
02 — The Supply Picture: A Structural Constraint, Not a Cycle

The most underappreciated driver of the 2026 outlook is the near-total absence of new office supply. Toronto's development pipeline has effectively reached zero for downtown product — and what little is coming is already fully spoken for. This is not a temporary lull. It is a multi-year structural constraint that will define market dynamics well into the decade.

Active Development Pipeline — Downtown Toronto

2026 Onwards

Construction starts hit multi-decade lows in 2025 — just 83,000 SF of new projects nationally in H2 2025. The national pipeline of 2.8M SF is 69.4% pre-leased. For practical purposes, the downtown Toronto office supply pipeline is empty beyond CIBC Square II.

Analyst View The development cycle that commenced in 2020 delivered over 9.7M SF of new downtown supply through 2025 — contributing to the vacancy spike that dominated headlines. That cycle is now definitively over. With no meaningful new supply on the horizon until 2029/30 at the earliest, the vacancy pressure of the past three years will not be replicated. This makes the current window — where cap rates are still wide and vacancy still elevated — potentially the last attractive entry point for core Toronto CBD office before the supply constraint fully prices in.

Conversion Activity — Removing Inventory, Not Adding It

Eight conversion projects removed over 1.0M SF of office inventory in Q4 2025 alone. While the majority of this activity is concentrated in Calgary, Toronto conversions — primarily Class C assets in Downtown East and peripheral locations — are quietly shrinking the overall inventory base. This dynamic further tightens effective supply of premium space by removing Class B/C product that might otherwise compete (however weakly) for tenants.

03 — Demand Dynamics: RTO, Large-Block Scarcity & The Broadening Tenant Base

Demand in Toronto's downtown office market has undergone a fundamental shift in 2025. What began as a flight-to-quality narrative driven by a handful of financial tenants has broadened into a multi-sector, RTO-driven demand story — and the convergence of accelerating RTO mandates with rapidly depleting large-block availability is creating the conditions for a structurally tighter leasing market in 2026.

🏦
RTO Mandates — The Big 5 Effect
Canada's five largest banks implemented four and five-day in-office requirements in 2025, directly driving the surge in Financial Core leasing. The Financial Core will set a new annual leasing record in 2025, surpassing the previous record of 2.5M SF set in 2024. RBC, Rogers, and Starbucks Canada all recalled staff in 2025/early 2026. Federal employees move to four days in-office from summer 2026 — adding government-covenant demand to an already tight premium market.
↑ Strongly Bullish for AAA/Class A
🔲
Large-Block Scarcity — Forcing Decisions
Only 15 contiguous blocks of over 100,000 SF remain available in downtown Toronto — and this figure is declining rapidly. Major tenants requiring large-format space face heightened competition and an increasingly binary choice: commit now or lose access to quality space. This dynamic is accelerating lease decisions that might otherwise have been deferred due to macro uncertainty, creating a self-reinforcing absorption cycle in H1 2026.
↑ Bullish — Accelerating Decisions
🌐
Broadening Tenant Demand
2025 leasing was dominated by financial institutions, but the tenant base is broadening. Wealthsimple, Lyft, and Nvidia secured significant downtown Toronto square footage in 2025. Tech, professional services, and life sciences tenants are increasingly active. CBRE's Q4 data confirms demand is "spreading across industry groups" — reducing concentration risk and supporting sustained absorption beyond the bank-driven cycle of the past two years.
↑ Bullish — Diversifying Demand
⚠️
Macro Uncertainty — Tariffs & Caution
US tariff risk and broader geopolitical uncertainty weighed on business confidence in 2025. However, the evidence suggests this is a decision-timing risk, not a demand-destruction risk. Tenants are delaying, not withdrawing. And critically — delayed decisions in a supply-constrained market actually worsen large-block scarcity, since occupiers hold onto premium space longer rather than releasing it. CUSMA protections have so far mitigated the worst-case tariff scenarios for Canadian business confidence.
→ Neutral — Timing Risk Only
The Scarcity Loop The interaction between RTO mandates and large-block scarcity creates a reinforcing dynamic that is underappreciated in most market commentary. As RTO mandates force tenants back into offices, the quality bar for acceptable space rises — employees will resist commuting to substandard environments. This directs demand exclusively toward AAA and well-amenitised Class A product. With only 15 large blocks remaining, tenants face a genuine scarcity constraint — and scarcity accelerates commitment. The result: absorption in the premium segment is likely to remain elevated in 2026 even if the macro environment softens.

04 — The Bifurcation: Premium vs. Commodity

The defining structural story of Toronto's downtown office market in 2026 is not recovery — it is bifurcation. The gap between AAA/Class A product and Class B/C is not cyclical softness on one side and cyclical strength on the other. It is a permanent reset of what tenants will accept, driven by RTO mandates that require employers to offer quality space to justify the commute.

AAA / Class A — In Upcycle

  • Class A vacancy fell 450 bps in 2025 alone
  • Avg. asking net rent hit $35.90/SF — 7-quarter high
  • AAA net effective rents beginning to climb

Class B/C — Structural Obsolescence
  • Class B/C vacancy at 25.4% — still rising YoY
  • Vacancy rising even as overall market tightens
  • Tenants relocating to Class A, not renewing in B/C
  • RTO mandates accelerating exodus — quality non-negotiable
  • Rent concessions failing to retain or attract tenants
  • Conversion is the most viable path for many assets
  • Investment volume declining — limited buyer appetite
  • Suburban B/C marginally better — North Yonge showing life
  • No structural catalyst for vacancy reversal in sight
The Permanent Reset Thesis Pre-pandemic, Class B/C space served a legitimate market function — it was cheaper, often well-located, and acceptable to cost-conscious tenants. The post-pandemic era has fundamentally altered this equation. Employers who are asking staff to commute four or five days a week are implicitly making a promise: the office will be worth the trip. A tired Class C floor plate in a building without amenities, cycling facilities, or quality end-of-trip infrastructure does not fulfil that promise. This is not about the economic cycle — it is about a permanent shift in what occupiers owe their employees in exchange for in-office attendance. Class B/C landlords who do not invest in repositioning their assets will face structural vacancy, not cyclical vacancy. The distinction matters enormously for underwriting.

Why We Lean Base Over Bull The fundamentals clearly support a continued tightening in the premium segment — supply is gone, demand is real, and RTO is accelerating. But the macro environment introduces meaningful uncertainty that we do not think the consensus is fully pricing in. US tariff risk, while partially mitigated by CUSMA, remains a live threat to business confidence — and business confidence, not supply and demand alone, determines the pace of leasing decisions. We lean base case not because we doubt the structural bull case, but because the timing of that bull case is genuinely uncertain in a tariff-driven macro environment. The premium trade is right — the question is whether it plays out in 12 months or 24.

The bifurcation thesis has direct and differentiated implications for capital deployment across the Toronto CBD office market. The investment narrative for 2026 is not a single market view — it is a highly asset-specific story where location, class, and lease quality will determine outcomes more than macro conditions.

The 70 York Precedent CBRE's sale of 70 York Street — 210,000 SF, 92.5% occupied, 6.5% cap rate, $639/SF — in November 2025 is the most instructive data point available for pricing stabilised downtown Toronto Class A office. At $639/SF and a 6.5% going-in cap rate, the deal was transacted at a meaningful discount to replacement cost. In a market where new supply is effectively zero until 2030, and where AAA vacancy is at 3.0%, the replacement cost argument for premium downtown office strengthens considerably over a 3–5 year hold. Investors underwriting similar assets today at 6.5% are buying into a market where cap rate compression is the most likely directional outcome under our base case.

08 — Conclusion
The Bottom Line
Two Markets. One Decision.
Toronto CBD office in 2026 will reward investors, occupiers, and analysts who understand that the aggregate headline number is irrelevant. The only question that matters is which side of the bifurcation you are on. AAA and well-positioned Class A product is entering a period of genuine scarcity-driven pricing power — a window that will remain open until the next development cycle delivers supply in 2029/30, and perhaps longer. Class B/C faces a structural reckoning that rent incentives alone cannot resolve. For investors, the thesis is clear: buy quality, at the right basis, now — before cap rate compression fully prices in the supply constraint. For occupiers, the message is equally clear: secure your space before the large-block market closes entirely. The 15 remaining contiguous blocks over 100,000 SF will not last the year.
